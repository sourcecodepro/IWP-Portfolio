About Me

I am an aerospace engineer and space architect, widely regarded as a pioneer of rocket and space technology. My career began in Germany, leading to the development of the V-2 rocket, the first long-range guided ballistic missile.

Later, I came to the United States, where I served as director of the Marshall Space Flight Center and was the chief architect of the Saturn V super heavy-lift launch vehicle, the rocket that propelled humanity to the Moon during the Apollo program.

My Work & Projects

Aggregat-4 (V-2 Rocket)

The world's first long-range guided ballistic missile.

Developed during the 1940s, the A-4 was a pioneering step in rocketry. It was the first artificial object to cross the boundary of space (Kármán line) and laid the essential groundwork for all future space launch vehicles.

Key Technologies:

  • Liquid Propulsion Engine
  • Inertial Guidance System
  • Supersonic Aerodynamics

PGM-11 Redstone

America's first large ballistic missile and first astronaut launch vehicle.

The Redstone was a direct descendant of the A-4. As a key project for the Army Ballistic Missile Agency (ABMA), it was modified into the Juno I launch vehicle, which launched America's first satellite (Explorer 1), and the Mercury-Redstone, which launched Alan Shepard, the first American in space.

Key Technologies:

  • Guided Missile Systems
  • Propulsion Uprating
  • Launch Vehicle Integration

Saturn V Launch Vehicle

The super heavy-lift rocket that took humanity to the Moon.

As the director of NASA's Marshall Space Flight Center, I led the design and development of the Saturn V. This three-stage rocket remains the most powerful launch vehicle ever successfully brought to operational status, successfully launching 24 astronauts to the Moon on the Apollo missions from 1968 to 1972.

Key Technologies:

  • Multi-Stage Propulsion
  • F-1 & J-2 Rocket Engines
  • Complex Vehicle Systems Engineering
